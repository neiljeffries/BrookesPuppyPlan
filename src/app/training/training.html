Cesar Millan's Training Methods

Proven techniques from the Dog Whisperer to raise a calm, balanced Yorkshire Terrier.

psychology Philosophy
psychology Core Philosophy Exercise, Discipline, Affection — in that order

Cesar's approach centres on understanding dogs as pack animals. Even a small Yorkshire Terrier needs a calm, assertive leader who provides structure and boundaries before affection. The formula is simple:

  1. Exercise — Drain your Yorkie's energy first through walks and play.
  2. Discipline — Set rules, boundaries, and limitations consistently — size doesn't excuse bad behaviour.
  3. Affection — Reward calm, submissive behaviour with love and treats.

library_books Methods
directions_walk The Walk

The structured walk is the foundation of Cesar's method. Walk your Yorkie beside or behind you — never in front. Even though they're small, letting them lead creates "small dog syndrome."

  • Use a lightweight harness to protect their delicate trachea
  • Keep a calm, forward-moving pace suited to their short stride
  • Correct pulling with a gentle sideways redirect
  • Walk for 20–30 minutes daily — Yorkies are energetic but tire faster than large breeds
 self_improvement Calm-Assertive Energy

Dogs mirror the energy of their owners. Yorkies are especially sensitive to your emotional state — Cesar teaches that projecting calm, assertive energy is the key to leadership. Anxious or excited energy creates an anxious or yappy Yorkie.

  • Stand tall with relaxed shoulders
  • Use steady, low-toned voice commands
  • Never correct your puppy when you're frustrated
  • Breathe and centre yourself before training sessions
 block Rules, Boundaries & Limitations

Dogs thrive with clear structure. Yorkies especially need consistent household rules from day one — their small size often leads owners to let bad behaviour slide, which creates bigger problems later.

  • Decide on no-go zones (furniture, certain rooms) — don't make exceptions because they're small
  • Make your Yorkie wait before meals and doorways
  • Don't allow jumping, nipping, or demand barking — common Yorkie habits to nip early
  • Every family member must enforce the same rules
 touch_app The "Tsst" Correction

Cesar's signature correction is a quick "tsst" sound paired with a calm finger touch to the neck or side. With a small breed like a Yorkie, be especially gentle — a light touch is all that's needed to interrupt their state.

  • Time the correction at the exact moment of the unwanted behaviour
  • Use minimal force — it's about interrupting the state, not punishment
  • Follow up by redirecting to a desired behaviour
  • Never use corrections when angry or emotional
 explore Nose, Eyes, Ears

Dogs experience the world in this order: nose first, then eyes, then ears. Cesar uses this to structure introductions and training.

  • Let your puppy sniff new things before expecting a reaction
  • Don't force eye contact — let it come naturally
  • Verbal commands are last, not first
  • Use scent-based enrichment to tire your puppy mentally
 group Pack Leadership

In Cesar's view, every household is a pack and your Yorkie needs to know their place in it. A Yorkie without a leader becomes yappy, possessive, or anxious — classic "small dog syndrome."

  • Eat before your dog and control food access
  • Enter and exit spaces first
  • Don't comfort fearful behaviour — redirect it
  • Claim your space by standing tall and stepping forward
 spa Rehabilitation vs. Training

Cesar distinguishes between training (teaching commands) and rehabilitation (fixing behavioural issues). Yorkie puppies mostly need training, but bad habits like excessive barking and resource guarding can start early.

  • Address fear, aggression, or anxiety immediately
  • Don't reinforce unwanted states with soothing words
  • Flooding (controlled exposure) can help with fearfulness
  • Patience and consistency are more important than any technique
 volunteer_activism Fulfillment Before Affection

Cesar stresses that giving affection at the wrong time can reinforce bad behaviour. Your Yorkie needs to feel fulfilled through exercise and mental stimulation before receiving love — no matter how adorable they look.

  • No affection during excited, anxious, or aggressive states
  • Reward calm, relaxed body language with gentle praise
  • Use treats sparingly — connection matters more than food
  • Quality time (calm sitting together) is the best reward
format_list_numbered Steps
1
Know Your Dog's Energy Level

Before any training begins, observe your Yorkie's energy level. Yorkshire Terriers are typically medium-to-high energy despite their small size. They're feisty, alert, and easily excited. This means they often need more mental stimulation than physical exhaustion before they can focus.
2
Exercise First — Always

Take your Yorkie on a structured walk before every training session. A tired Yorkie is a focused Yorkie. This drains excess energy and puts them in a calm, receptive state. For a Yorkshire Terrier, 15–20 minutes of brisk walking is usually enough — don't overdo it with their small legs.
3
Project Calm-Assertive Energy

Centre yourself before engaging. Stand tall, breathe slowly, and keep your body relaxed but confident. Your Yorkie reads your body language and emotional state before anything else. Yorkies are exceptionally attuned to their owner's mood — if you're tense or frustrated, they'll become anxious or defiant.
4
Set Clear Rules & Boundaries

Decide on the specific behaviour you want to address. Communicate the boundary through body language and spatial pressure — step forward, claim your space, and use a calm "tsst" to interrupt unwanted behaviour. Be consistent: the rule is always the rule, not just sometimes.
5
Correct at the Right Moment

Timing is everything. Correct your Yorkie at the exact instant the unwanted behaviour starts — not after. A late correction confuses any dog. With Yorkies, use the lightest correction possible: a soft sound or gentle spatial pressure. Their small size means a little goes a long way.
6
Redirect to the Desired Behaviour

After correcting, immediately guide your puppy toward what you do want. If they were jumping, ask for a sit. If they were pulling, redirect their focus forward. Nature abhors a vacuum — don't just stop a behaviour, replace it with the right one.
7
Wait for the Calm State

Do not reward until your Yorkie is in a calm, submissive state. Watch for relaxed ears, a low or neutral tail, soft eyes, and a loose body. This is the moment your dog is truly learning. Yorkies are expert manipulators with their cute faces — don't let puppy eyes fool you into rewarding an excited or demanding state.
8
Reward with Affection

Once your Yorkie is calm and balanced, reward them with gentle affection — a soft touch, calm praise, or a tiny treat. This is Cesar's third pillar. Affection given at the right time seals the lesson and strengthens the bond between you and your Yorkie.
9
Repeat with Patience & Consistency

Dogs learn through repetition, not explanation. Practice the same routine daily. Every family member must follow the same rules — Yorkies are smart enough to test each person for weak spots. Cesar says it takes a dog as little as 10 minutes to learn a new behaviour — but it takes humans much longer to be consistent enough to make it stick.
menu_book Guides
front_hand Sit
1
Exercise First

Take your Yorkie on a brisk 15–20 minute walk before the session. A dog full of pent-up energy cannot focus. You need your Yorkie in a calm, receptive state before asking for any command. Even though they're small, Yorkshire Terriers have plenty of energy to burn.
2
Claim Your Space

Stand tall and calm. Project calm-assertive energy. Your Yorkie should already be looking to you for direction. If they're distracted or hyper, they're not ready — walk more or wait. Yorkies can be stubborn, so patience here is key.
3
Use Body Language First

Hold a tiny treat above and slightly behind your Yorkie's head. Their natural response is to look up and sit down. Do not say "sit" yet — let the body movement happen first. Use pea-sized treats — Yorkies are tiny and can gain weight quickly. Cesar teaches nose, eyes, ears: the dog experiences the scent and visual cue before the verbal one.
4
Mark the Moment

The instant their bottom touches the ground, give calm praise — a gentle "good" — and offer the treat. Timing is critical. Rewarding even one second late teaches the wrong thing.
5
Add the Word

Once your Yorkie reliably sits from the hand gesture alone (after several sessions), add the word "sit" just before the hand motion. The word becomes associated with the action they already know. Never repeat the command — say it once, wait, and guide if needed. Yorkies are clever and will learn to ignore repeated commands quickly.
6
Phase Out Treats

Gradually reduce treats and replace with calm affection. Cesar emphasises that the connection with the owner should be the ultimate reward, not food. Yorkies can become treat-obsessed, so transition to praise and gentle touch as soon as the behaviour is consistent.
pan_tool Stay
1
Master "Sit" First

"Stay" builds on "sit." Your Yorkie must be able to sit reliably on command before attempting this. If the foundation isn't solid, go back to the sit training. Yorkies are naturally restless, so "stay" requires extra patience.
2
Ask for Sit, Then Hold

Ask your Yorkie to sit. Once they're sitting calmly, hold your open palm facing them (a "stop" gesture) and take one small step back. Wait just 2–3 seconds at first. If they stay, return and reward with calm affection.
3
Correct Breaks Immediately

If your Yorkie breaks the stay, use a calm "tsst" and guide them back to the exact spot. Do not show frustration. Reset and try again with a shorter duration. The correction must come at the instant they start to move, not after they've already gotten up. Be gentle — Yorkies are sensitive to harsh corrections.
4
Increase Duration Gradually

Add a few seconds at a time. Go from 3 seconds to 5, then 10, then 20. Over days, build up to minutes. Never rush — if your Yorkie fails repeatedly, you've advanced too quickly. Drop back to where they were succeeding.
5
Add Distance

Once your Yorkie holds a stay for 30+ seconds with you one step away, gradually increase your distance. Take two steps, then three, then walk across the room. Always return to your Yorkie to reward — never call them to you, as that teaches them to break the stay.
6
Add Distractions

The final challenge is proofing against distractions. Practice in different rooms, then outside. Have someone walk by. Drop a toy nearby. Each distraction tests your Yorkie's self-control. Correct breaks calmly and reset. A Yorkie that can stay amidst distractions is a balanced dog.
pets Come
1
Start Indoors with No Distractions

Begin in a quiet room with no competing stimuli. Your Yorkie should already be in a calm state — if they're wound up, take them on a walk first. Cesar says recall training fails outdoors when it hasn't been mastered indoors first. Yorkies have an independent streak, so start in the most controlled environment possible.
2
Use Body Language Before Words

Crouch down to your Yorkie's level and open your body posture — this is an invitation dogs understand instinctively. Tap your knees or clap gently. When your Yorkie moves toward you, then introduce the word "come." Nose, eyes, ears — the physical cue comes first, the verbal cue last.
3
Reward the Arrival — Every Time

The instant your Yorkie reaches you, give calm praise and a tiny treat. Coming to you must always be the best thing that happens to them. Cesar warns: never call your dog to you and then do something they dislike (bath, crate, leaving the park). That teaches them "come" means something bad happens.
4
Add Distance Gradually

Start just a few feet away. Once your Yorkie comes reliably at close range, move to the other side of the room, then another room, then a hallway. Each increase in distance is a new challenge — don't skip steps. If they start ignoring you, you've gone too far too fast. Yorkies can be wilful, so keep sessions short (5–10 minutes) to maintain engagement.
5
Use a Long Line Outdoors

When you move outside, use a 15–20 foot training lead (lighter weight for a small breed). This gives your Yorkie the feeling of freedom while you maintain control. Let them explore, then call "come" once — calmly and clearly. If they don't respond, gently guide them in with the line. Never chase your Yorkie — that turns it into a game they'll always win.
6
Never Repeat the Command

Cesar is adamant: say "come" once. If you say it five times, you're teaching your dog that the first four don't count. Say it once, wait, and if they don't respond, use the long line to guide them. One command, one response — that's the standard.
7
Proof Against Distractions

The ultimate test is calling your Yorkie away from something exciting — another dog, food on the ground, a squirrel. Build up to this slowly. Practice near mild distractions first, then gradually increase the difficulty. A reliable recall in any situation could save your Yorkie's life — especially important for a small breed that's vulnerable to larger animals and traffic.
volume_off Stop Barking
1
Identify the Cause

Cesar says barking is a symptom, not the problem. Yorkies are one of the most vocal breeds — they bark from boredom, anxiety, territorial instinct, excitement, or simply because they can. Each root cause needs a different energy from you. An under-exercised Yorkie barks because they have no outlet — the fix is the walk, not correcting the bark.
2
Exercise to Drain Energy

Most nuisance barking stems from excess energy. A well-exercised Yorkie is a quieter Yorkie. Before addressing the barking itself, make sure your Yorkie is getting sufficient daily exercise — 15–25 minutes of walking plus indoor play. A tired Yorkie has far less to bark about.
3
Stay Calm — Never Yell

Yelling at a barking Yorkie is the worst thing you can do. Your Yorkie interprets your shouting as you joining in — and Yorkies love a good bark-along. Project calm-assertive energy. Your silence and composed body language will do more than any loud command.
4
Claim the Trigger

If your Yorkie barks at the door or window, step between them and the trigger. Claim that space with your body. Move toward your Yorkie, not away. Use a calm "tsst" and spatial pressure to push them back from the trigger. You're telling them: "I've got this, you don't need to alert." Yorkies often appoint themselves as guard dogs — let them know that's your job.
5
Wait for Silence, Then Reward

Do not give any attention — positive or negative — while your Yorkie is barking. Wait for even a brief moment of silence. The instant they stop, reward with calm praise. You're teaching them that silence earns attention, barking earns nothing. With Yorkies, this takes real commitment — they can bark for a long time.
6
Be Consistent Every Time

Every family member must respond the same way. If one person yells and another ignores it, your Yorkie gets mixed signals. Consistency is the key to breaking the cycle. Cesar says most barking problems are resolved within days when the entire household commits to calm, consistent correction — but with Yorkies, expect it to take a bit longer. They're tenacious.
do_not_step Stop Jumping
1
Understand Why They Jump

Yorkies jump to seek attention and to greet at face level — and because they're so small, people often pick them up or laugh, which rewards the behaviour. When you respond with excitement, pushing, or even telling them "no" in a high-pitched voice, you're reinforcing the jumping. Cesar teaches that jumping is almost always the owner's fault — we've trained them that jumping works.
2
No Touch, No Talk, No Eye Contact

When your Yorkie jumps on you, give them absolutely nothing. Turn your body sideways, fold your arms, look away, and say nothing. This is Cesar's "no touch, no talk, no eye contact" rule. You become boring. The jumping loses its payoff. This is especially hard with Yorkies because they're adorable — but resist.
3
Claim Your Space

If turning away isn't enough, step calmly into your Yorkie's space. Move forward, not backward. Use your body to push them back without touching them. A calm "tsst" at the moment they begin to leap interrupts the behaviour before it completes. Be gentle with your movement — they're small and can be knocked over easily.
4
Wait for Four on the Floor

Only greet your Yorkie when all four paws are on the ground and they are in a calm state. This may take patience — they may jump several times before giving up. The moment they stand or sit calmly, get low to their level, and give gentle, calm affection. You're teaching them: "calm gets love, jumping gets nothing."
5
Practice with Guests

The hardest test is when visitors arrive. Ask guests to follow the same rules: ignore the jumping, turn away, no excited greetings. Put your Yorkie on a leash during arrivals if needed. Make them sit before the guest engages. Cesar says the first 5 minutes of a guest's arrival determine the dog's behaviour for the entire visit. Warn guests not to "baby" your Yorkie — this undoes all your work.
6
Exercise Before Social Situations

If you know guests are coming, take your Yorkie on a walk beforehand. A well-exercised Yorkie has far less impulse to jump. Combine this with the "no touch, no talk, no eye contact" rule, and jumping disappears within weeks.
grass Potty Training
1
Establish a Routine

Cesar emphasises that dogs are creatures of routine. Take your Yorkie outside at the same times every day: first thing in the morning, after meals, after naps, after play, and last thing at night. Yorkies have tiny bladders, so take them out more frequently than you would a larger breed — every 1–2 hours for young pups.
2
Pick a Designated Spot

Always take your Yorkie to the same area outside. The scent from previous visits tells them "this is the bathroom." Walk them on a leash to the spot — don't let them wander and play first. This is a business trip, not a field trip. In bad weather, Yorkies are more likely to refuse to go outside, so be firm and consistent.
3
Reward Immediately

The instant your Yorkie finishes going in the right spot, give calm praise and a tiny treat. Not 10 seconds later — immediately. They need to connect the act of going in that spot with the reward. Cesar says timing is everything in dog psychology.
4
Supervise Constantly Indoors

When inside, keep your Yorkie in the same room as you. Watch for signs they need to go: sniffing the floor, circling, squatting. The moment you see these signs, calmly pick them up and carry them outside to the designated spot. Yorkies are small enough to scoop up quickly — use this to your advantage. Prevention is easier than correction.
5
Never Punish Accidents

Cesar is clear: never rub a dog's nose in it or yell after an accident. If you didn't catch them in the act, the moment has passed — punishment after the fact creates fear and confusion, not learning. Clean it up with an enzymatic cleaner (critical for Yorkies — if they can still smell it, they'll go there again) and recommit to supervision.
6
Use Crate Training as a Tool

Dogs instinctively avoid soiling their sleeping area. A properly sized crate for your Yorkie (big enough to stand and turn, not bigger) helps develop bladder control. Take them straight outside when you open the crate. Cesar views the crate as a den — a safe, natural space — never as punishment. For Yorkies, ensure the crate isn't too large or they may designate one corner as a toilet.
7
Use a Training Mat as a Backup

A puppy training mat (or pee pad) can be a valuable indoor safety net — especially for a tiny breed like a Yorkie. Place the mat in one consistent spot away from their sleeping and eating areas. Treat it the same way you treat the outdoor spot: lead your Yorkie to the mat on a schedule, wait calmly, and reward the instant they use it.

  • Use a mat with an absorbent core and leak-proof backing to protect your floors
  • Keep the mat in the same location — moving it confuses your puppy
  • Transition gradually from mat to outdoor-only by moving the mat closer to the door over time, then removing it once outdoor habits are solid
  • A mat is especially useful overnight, during bad weather, or when you can't get outside quickly enough for a tiny Yorkie bladder
  • Never use the mat as a permanent replacement for outdoor training — the goal is always to get your Yorkie going outside reliably
8
Be Patient — It Takes Weeks

Yorkshire Terriers are notoriously one of the hardest breeds to house-train. Most Yorkies take 4–6 months or even longer to be fully reliable. Cesar reminds owners that setbacks are normal and not a sign of failure. Stay calm, stay consistent, and trust the process. Small bladders and stubborn temperaments mean this takes longer than with larger breeds — but it absolutely can be done.
directions_walk Heel Walk
1
Practice Leash Skills Indoors First

Before you ever step outside, clip the leash on inside the house. Walk your Yorkie through hallways and around rooms. This low-distraction environment is where they learn to follow your lead without competing smells, sounds, or other dogs. Cesar says if your dog can't walk beside you in the living room, they're not ready for the street.
2
Master the Door Ritual

The walk starts before you open the front door. Put the leash on calmly — if your Yorkie goes wild with excitement, stand still and wait. Do not open the door until they are sitting or standing calmly beside you. Then you walk through first, and invite them to follow. Cesar says whoever goes through the door first leads the walk — it must always be you.
3
Claim the Hallway & Indoor Spaces

Walk through every doorway and narrow space in your home first. Change direction without warning. Stop suddenly and wait for your Yorkie to stop and look at you. These indoor exercises build the habit of paying attention to you and establish that you control the movement. Once your Yorkie follows you calmly through the house without pulling, lunging, or forging ahead, they're ready for the real walk.
4
Understand the Pack Walk

In Cesar's world, the walk is the single most important activity. A Yorkie that walks beside or behind you is acknowledging you as the pack leader. A Yorkie that pulls ahead believes they are leading — and Yorkies definitely think they're bigger than they are. This isn't about obedience — it's about the relationship dynamic.
5
Use the Right Equipment

For a Yorkshire Terrier, use a well-fitted harness rather than a collar — Yorkies are prone to tracheal collapse, and any pressure on the neck can be dangerous. Keep the leash short for control, but avoid tension. A lightweight leash works best for their small frame. Never use a retractable leash — these teach pulling.
6
Set the Pace — You Lead

You decide the speed, direction, and when to stop. Walk with purpose and confidence. Don't let your Yorkie dictate the route by sniffing every bush. Sniff breaks are earned at your discretion. Keep in mind your Yorkie's small stride — slow your pace slightly so they can walk comfortably beside you without having to trot.
7
Correct Pulling Instantly

The moment the leash goes tight, give a gentle sideways redirect (not a tug — Yorkies are delicate) and a calm "tsst." Then immediately resume walking. Don't stop and wait — keep moving. The correction should snap your Yorkie out of the pulling state, not yank them back. Be mindful of their small size — corrections should be light.
8
Change Directions Frequently

Keep your Yorkie guessing by changing direction often — especially at first. Turn left, turn right, do a U-turn. This forces your Yorkie to pay attention to you rather than the environment. When they start watching you for cues, you've gained their focus.
9
Reward the Heel Position

When your Yorkie walks calmly beside you with a loose leash, give quiet praise or an occasional tiny treat at your side. Don't make a big fuss — calm energy reinforces calm behaviour. They should learn that being next to you is the most comfortable, rewarding place to be.
10
Practice Daily — No Shortcuts

Cesar says the walk must happen every single day, rain or shine. It's not optional exercise — it's the core of your leadership. For a Yorkshire Terrier, aim for 20–30 minutes of structured walking per day. Their small legs cover less ground, but the mental benefits of a daily pack walk are the same as for any breed.
house Crate Training
1
Choose the Right Crate

Pick a crate just large enough for your Yorkie to stand, turn around, and lie down — no bigger. Too much space lets them designate a toilet corner. For a Yorkshire Terrier, a small travel crate or wire crate with a divider works best. Cesar views the crate as a den — a natural, safe space that fulfils your dog's instinct to seek shelter.
2
Introduce the Crate with Scent

Remember: nose, eyes, ears. Place the crate in a common area with the door open and a familiar blanket inside. Let your Yorkie sniff and explore it on their own terms — never force them in. Drop a few tiny treats just inside the door. Cesar says the crate must be discovered, not imposed. Yorkies are curious and will investigate when the pressure is off.
3
Feed Meals in the Crate

Start placing your Yorkie's food bowl inside the crate. At first, right at the entrance — then gradually move it deeper inside. This builds a positive association. Your Yorkie learns: good things happen in the crate. Once they're eating comfortably inside, gently close the door while they eat, then open it the moment they finish.
4
Build Duration Gradually

After meals, begin closing the door for short periods — 1 minute, then 3, then 5, then 10. Stay in the room and project calm energy. If your Yorkie whines, do not open the door until they stop — even for a brief pause. Opening the door during whining teaches them that noise gets results. Cesar is clear: reward silence, ignore noise.
5
Leave the Room

Once your Yorkie is calm with the door closed for 10+ minutes while you're present, start leaving the room briefly. Step out for 30 seconds, then return calmly — no fanfare. Gradually extend your absence. Yorkies are prone to separation anxiety, so keep departures and returns low-key. Cesar says: no emotional goodbyes, no excited hellos.
6
Exercise Before Crate Time

Always drain your Yorkie's energy before expecting them to rest in the crate. A 15–20 minute walk or play session puts them in a calm, tired state — making the crate feel like a welcome rest stop rather than a prison. Cesar's formula applies here: exercise first, then the crate becomes discipline (structure), then affection when they're calm inside.
7
Never Use the Crate as Punishment

Cesar is adamant: the crate must always be a positive space. Never send your Yorkie to the crate when you're angry or as a consequence for bad behaviour. If the crate becomes associated with punishment, your Yorkie will resist it and the training falls apart. The crate is a den — their safe haven, not a timeout corner.
8
Set a Nighttime Routine

Place the crate in or near your bedroom at night so your Yorkie doesn't feel isolated. Take them outside for a final toilet trip, then calmly guide them into the crate. Yorkies have tiny bladders — expect to take young puppies out once during the night for the first few weeks. As they grow, they'll hold it through the night. Consistency and patience are everything.
pets Down
1
Exercise First

Always drain your Yorkie's energy before training. A 15–20 minute walk puts them in a calm, receptive state. Cesar says you should never try to teach a hyper dog — a tired Yorkie is a focused Yorkie. This is especially important for "Down" because it requires your dog to surrender to a vulnerable, submissive position.
2
Start from a Sit

Your Yorkie should already know "Sit" reliably. Ask for a sit, then hold a small treat in front of their nose. Cesar emphasises building commands in layers — "Down" is an extension of "Sit", not a separate trick. If your Yorkie doesn't sit on cue yet, go back and practise that first.
3
Lure Downward with the Treat

From the sit position, slowly move the treat straight down from your Yorkie's nose to the floor between their front paws. Their body should follow into a down. Yorkies are small, so keep your movements slow and close — big gestures can startle them. Don't push their body down; let the nose lead. Cesar says: nose, eyes, ears — always work with scent first.
4
Mark and Reward Instantly

The moment your Yorkie's elbows touch the floor, say "Down" in a calm, firm tone and give the treat immediately. Timing is everything — even a one-second delay and your Yorkie won't connect the behaviour with the reward. Use tiny, soft treats since Yorkies have small stomachs and you'll be repeating this many times.
5
Add the Verbal Cue

Once your Yorkie is consistently following the lure into a down, start saying "Down" just before the hand motion. Over several sessions, your Yorkie will associate the word with the action. Use a calm, low tone — Yorkies are sensitive to vocal energy and a sharp command can make them anxious rather than obedient. Cesar's calm-assertive energy applies here.
6
Fade the Lure

Gradually reduce the hand movement — use a smaller gesture, then just a pointed finger toward the floor. The goal is for your Yorkie to respond to "Down" with voice alone. Yorkies are clever and will keep expecting the treat in your hand if you don't fade it. Cesar says: the treat is a tool, not a bribe.
7
Build Duration

Ask for "Down" and wait a beat longer before treating — 2 seconds, then 5, then 10. Your Yorkie needs to learn that "Down" means stay down, not touch the floor and pop back up. If they break the position, calmly reset and try again. Yorkies can be impatient, so keep sessions short — 5 minutes maximum — and end on a success.
8
Practise in Different Locations

Once reliable at home, practise "Down" in the garden, on walks, and around distractions. Cesar says a command your dog only follows in the living room isn't a command — it's a suggestion. Yorkies are easily overstimulated by new scents and sounds, so raise the difficulty gradually. Always project calm-assertive energy and reward compliance.
do_not_touch Leave It
1
Understand Why Yorkies Need This

Yorkies are low to the ground with a strong terrier nose — they hoover up everything on walks. "Leave It" isn't optional for a toy breed; ingesting the wrong thing can cause a blockage or poisoning that's life-threatening for a dog this small. Cesar teaches that impulse control starts with calm-assertive leadership, not shouting.
2
Start with a Treat in Your Closed Fist

Hold a small treat in your closed hand and present it to your Yorkie. They'll sniff, lick, and paw at your hand. Wait. The moment they pull back or look away, say "Leave it" and reward with a different treat from your other hand. Cesar says patience wins — let the dog make the choice, then reward the right one.
3
Move to an Open Palm

Once your Yorkie consistently backs off from a closed fist, try an open palm. If they lunge, close your hand quickly. If they hold back, mark it and reward from the other hand. Yorkies are clever and will test boundaries — stay consistent. Never let them get the bait treat, or the exercise loses its meaning.
4
Place the Treat on the Floor

Put a treat on the floor and cover it with your hand. Say "Leave it." When your Yorkie backs off, reward from your other hand. Gradually lift your hand away from the floor treat. If they dart for it, cover it again — no scolding, just block and reset. This mirrors real-world scenarios where food is on the ground.
5
Add Distance

Place the treat on the floor, say "Leave it," and take a step back. If your Yorkie stays, return and reward. Gradually increase the distance. Yorkies tend to break when you move away because they're companion dogs who fixate on your position — this step builds true impulse control independent of your proximity.
6
Practise on Walks

On a lead, when your Yorkie lunges toward something on the pavement, give a calm "Leave it" and redirect with gentle lead pressure. The moment they disengage, reward. Use a harness rather than a collar — Yorkies have delicate tracheas and collar corrections can cause a collapsed trachea. Cesar says: redirect, don't punish.
7
Generalise to Other Objects

Practise "Leave it" with toys, tissues, socks, and other dogs' food bowls — not just treats. Yorkies are notorious sock thieves and tissue shredders. The command should mean "ignore that thing completely," regardless of what it is. Vary the objects so your Yorkie learns the concept, not just the specific setup.
groups Socialisation with Larger Dogs
1
Understand Small Dog Syndrome

Yorkies often bark, lunge, and growl at larger dogs — not because they're brave, but because they're fearful. Owners make it worse by picking them up at every encounter, which reinforces the anxiety. Cesar is clear: carrying your dog away teaches them the world is dangerous. Your Yorkie needs to face it with you as a calm leader.
2
Exercise Before Introductions

Never introduce your Yorkie to a larger dog when they're full of pent-up energy. Walk them for 15–20 minutes first so they arrive in a calm state. Cesar's formula — exercise, discipline, affection — applies here directly. A tired Yorkie is far less likely to react with fear-aggression than a wired one.
3
Start at a Distance

Begin with your Yorkie at a distance where they can see the larger dog but aren't reacting. This is their "threshold." Reward calm behaviour with treats. Gradually reduce the distance over multiple sessions. If your Yorkie starts barking or pulling, you've moved too close too fast — back up and try again.
4
Parallel Walking

Walk alongside the larger dog with a comfortable gap between them — both dogs moving in the same direction. This is how dogs naturally get acquainted without confrontation. Cesar uses this technique constantly. Keep the lead loose; tension on the lead transfers your anxiety straight to your Yorkie. Walk with confidence.
5
Allow a Controlled Sniff

When both dogs are calm during parallel walking, allow a brief side-by-side sniff — nose to flank, not face to face. Keep it to 3 seconds, then calmly walk on. Nose, eyes, ears: scent comes first. If either dog stiffens or your Yorkie starts to growl, calmly redirect and try again later. Never force the interaction.
6
Don't Pick Them Up

This is the hardest rule for Yorkie owners. When your small dog reacts, your instinct is to scoop them up. But Cesar says this elevates them above the other dog (literally and psychologically) and confirms there's something to fear. Instead, step between the dogs calmly and redirect your Yorkie with a lead correction and forward motion.
7
Choose the Right Playmates

Not every large dog is safe for a 5-pound Yorkie. Look for calm, well-socialised dogs with low prey drive. A gentle Labrador is very different from a high-drive terrier. Always supervise — even friendly play can injure a toy breed if the larger dog is too boisterous. Cesar says: know the energy of the dog you're introducing.
8
Build Positive Associations Over Time

Socialisation isn't a single event — it's an ongoing process. Expose your Yorkie to calm larger dogs regularly so they learn bigger dogs aren't threats. Reward calm behaviour every time. Over weeks, your Yorkie will stop reacting and start ignoring or even enjoying the company of bigger dogs. Patience and consistency are everything.
content_cut Handling & Grooming Desensitisation
1
Why This Matters for Yorkies

Yorkies have a long, silky coat that needs daily brushing, regular trimming, topknot care, and frequent dental attention. If your puppy isn't comfortable being handled, grooming becomes a battle for life. Cesar says: the time to desensitise is now, while they're young and impressionable. Starting early prevents years of stress for both of you.
2
Handle Paws Daily

Gently hold each paw for a few seconds while your Yorkie is in a calm state — after exercise, never when they're hyper. Touch each toe, press lightly on the pads, and spread the toes gently. Reward with a tiny treat after each paw. This prepares them for nail trimming, which Yorkies often despise. Keep sessions under 2 minutes.
3
Touch Ears and Mouth

Yorkies are prone to dental disease and ear infections. Lift each ear flap and gently touch inside. Lift their lip and touch their gums and teeth. Go slowly — if your Yorkie pulls away, you've pushed too fast. Cesar says: claim the space calmly, don't chase the dog. Return to it when they settle. Reward acceptance, not resistance.
4
Introduce the Brush

Let your Yorkie sniff the brush first — nose, eyes, ears. Then gently stroke their back with it, just a few passes. Don't tackle knots or mats in this stage; just build a positive association with the tool. Reward calm acceptance. Over several sessions, gradually work toward the legs, chest, and face — the areas Yorkies resist most.
5
Simulate Grooming Tools

Run an electric toothbrush (switched off, then on) near your Yorkie so they get used to vibrations and buzzing. Touch closed scissors to their coat without cutting. Hold a hair dryer at a distance on a low setting. Each tool gets its own slow introduction. Cesar's principle applies: never force the experience, let the dog adjust at their own pace.
6
Practise the Grooming Table Position

Place your Yorkie on a raised, non-slip surface (a table with a towel works). Ask them to stand still for a few seconds, then reward. Build duration gradually. Professional groomers use elevated tables, so getting your Yorkie comfortable with this position now saves enormous stress later. Never leave them unattended on a raised surface.
7
Keep Sessions Short and Positive

Desensitisation sessions should be 3–5 minutes maximum. Always end on a good note — if your Yorkie is struggling with ear handling, go back to something easy like paw touches, reward, and stop. Cesar says: you set the energy. If you're frustrated, your Yorkie feels it. Walk away, reset, and try again tomorrow. Consistency beats intensity.
weekend Place / Settle on a Mat
1
Choose a Designated Spot

Pick a small mat, blanket, or bed — something portable that becomes your Yorkie's "place." It should be big enough for them to lie down comfortably but small enough to take anywhere. Cesar views "Place" as teaching your dog to switch off and relax on command — it gives them psychological boundaries and reduces anxiety.
2
Lure onto the Mat

Place the mat on the floor and lure your Yorkie onto it with a treat. The moment all four paws are on the mat, say "Place" and reward. Repeat 5–10 times. Yorkies catch on quickly because they're food-motivated — but they'll also try to grab the treat and run. Only reward when they're standing or sitting on the mat, not leaving it.
3
Add a Down on the Mat

Once your Yorkie goes to the mat reliably, ask for a "Down" once they're on it. Reward the down position. "Place" should ultimately mean "go to your mat and lie down calmly" — not just stand on it. This builds on the Down command, so make sure that's solid first. Cesar says: layer commands, don't rush ahead.
4
Build Duration

Ask your Yorkie to go to their place and lie down. Wait 5 seconds before rewarding. Then 10, then 30, then a minute. If they break, calmly guide them back — no fuss, no frustration. Yorkies are velcro dogs who want to be with you constantly. "Place" teaches them that being calm independently is also rewarding.
5
Add Distance

With your Yorkie settled on the mat, take one step back. If they stay, return and reward. Gradually increase distance — across the room, into another room briefly. This is tough for Yorkies who suffer from separation anxiety. Go slowly. Cesar says: the dog needs to trust that you'll always come back. Build that trust in small increments.
6
Add Distractions

Practise "Place" while the doorbell rings, while you eat dinner, or when visitors arrive. Yorkies are alert dogs that react to every sound — "Place" gives them a job to do instead of barking at the door. Start with mild distractions and build up. If they break, guide them back calmly. No scolding, just repetition.
7
Take It on the Road

Bring the mat to a café, a friend's house, or the vet waiting room. Your Yorkie should recognise it as their calm spot anywhere. This is incredibly useful for a toy breed that goes everywhere with you. Cesar says: the mat becomes a portable "state of mind" — your dog sees it and knows it's time to settle.
back_hand Drop It / Give
1
Why Yorkies Need This

Yorkies are notorious for stealing socks, tissues, and small objects — then running off with them. Their small size makes it tempting to just grab things from their mouth, but this builds resource guarding. Cesar says: never chase a dog for an object. That turns it into a game they'll repeat. Teach a trade instead.
2
Start with a Low-Value Toy

Give your Yorkie a toy they like but aren't obsessed with. Let them hold it for a few seconds, then present a high-value treat near their nose. When they open their mouth to take the treat, say "Drop it" and pick up the toy. Give the treat immediately. Then give the toy back — this teaches them that dropping something doesn't mean losing it.
3
Always Trade Up

The treat must be more valuable than what they're holding. For Yorkies, tiny pieces of chicken or cheese work well. If the trade isn't worth it, your Yorkie will clamp down harder. Cesar's principle: make the right choice easy and the wrong choice unrewarding. Never prise their jaws open — that creates a dog who bites to protect items.
4
Increase the Value of the Object

Once your Yorkie drops low-value toys reliably, practise with higher-value items — a favourite chew, a stolen sock, a tissue. The trade-up treat needs to be exceptional for these. Yorkies are stubborn and will hold onto prised possessions if the reward isn't compelling enough. Be patient and keep the exchange positive.
5
Add the Verbal Cue Without a Treat in Hand

Say "Drop it" before showing the treat. Give your Yorkie a moment to respond to the words alone. If they drop, reward from your pocket. If they don't, show the treat as a prompt. Over time, they should respond to the verbal cue first. Cesar says: the treat is a bridge to the behaviour, not a permanent bribe.
6
Practise During Play

During a tug game, pause and say "Drop it." When your Yorkie releases, reward and resume the game. The game itself becomes the reward. This makes "Drop it" part of normal play rather than something that only happens when they've stolen something they shouldn't have. Yorkies love tug — use that motivation.
7
Never Chase

If your Yorkie grabs something and runs, do not chase them. Turn away, crouch down, and call them in an upbeat tone — or offer a trade. Chasing a Yorkie around the house reinforces the "keep-away" game and turns every stolen item into an invitation to play. Cesar says: you can't correct what you're chasing. Stay calm, redirect, trade.
directions_run Recall Under Distraction
1
The Terrier Prey Drive Problem

Yorkies are terriers — bred to chase rats. That prey drive doesn't vanish because they're small and cute. A Yorkie who spots a squirrel, a blowing leaf, or a running child can bolt and ignore everything you shout. Cesar says: you need to be more interesting than the distraction. This guide builds that reliability.
2
Master Indoor Recall First

Before adding distractions, your Yorkie's "Come" must be rock-solid indoors. Call them from another room, reward generously when they arrive. Practise 10 times a day in short bursts. If they don't come reliably in a quiet house, they won't come at a park. Cesar says: don't raise the difficulty until the foundation is unshakeable.
3
Use a Long Line

Attach a 5-metre training lead and practise recall in the garden. Let your Yorkie explore, then call them. If they come, massive reward. If they ignore you, gently guide them in with the line — no yanking, Yorkies have fragile tracheas. The long line prevents them from learning they can ignore you while keeping them safe.
4
Add Mild Distractions

Practise recall while a family member walks past, while a toy is on the ground, or near a mildly interesting scent. Call your Yorkie away from the distraction. If they come, reward with something exceptional — the reward for leaving a distraction must be better than the distraction itself. Yorkie-sized treats should be tiny but high-value.
5
Increase Distraction Difficulty Gradually

Move to busier environments: a quiet park, then a busy park, then near other dogs. Always use the long line until recall is reliable at each level. Yorkies get overstimulated quickly — new smells and sounds overwhelm their small brains. If they fail at a level, go back to the previous one. Cesar says: set your dog up to succeed.
6
Never Call to Punish

If your Yorkie finally comes after ignoring you three times, reward them anyway. Never scold a dog that eventually comes to you — you'll poison the command. "Come" must always equal good things. Cesar says: the recall must be the best thing that happens to your dog in that moment. If you're angry, take a breath before they reach you.
7
Practise the Emergency Recall

Choose a special word — something you never use normally — and pair it with an extraordinary reward (real chicken, a special toy). Practise it rarely so it stays powerful. This is your emergency brake for when your Yorkie chases something toward a road. For a 5-pound dog with no road sense, this could genuinely save their life.
directions_car Car Travel Training
1
Why Yorkies Struggle with Cars

Toy breeds are more prone to car anxiety and motion sickness than larger dogs. The vibrations feel amplified in a small body, and the moving world outside the window can overwhelm them. Cesar says: the car should be an extension of your calm energy. If you're tense about their behaviour, they'll mirror it. Approach car training with patient leadership.
2
Start with the Parked Car

Sit in the parked car with your Yorkie. Engine off. Let them sniff around and settle. Reward calm behaviour. Do this for 5 minutes a day for several days. Cesar's nose-eyes-ears approach applies — let your dog explore the space with scent first. If they're panicking in a stationary car, don't move to the next step. Wait until they're relaxed.
3
Engine On, No Movement

Turn the engine on and sit. Let your Yorkie adjust to the vibrations and noise. Reward calm behaviour. Some Yorkies will tremble — don't soothe them with affection, as Cesar warns this reinforces the fearful state. Instead, project calm indifference. When the engine becomes a non-event for them, you're ready to move.
4
Short Drives Only

Drive to the end of the road and back — that's it. Then get out and have a brief walk. The walk is the reward. Gradually increase distance over days and weeks. If your Yorkie is sick, keep trips shorter and consult your vet about motion sickness. Yorkies have sensitive stomachs, and travelling on an empty stomach can help.
5
Use a Secure Carrier or Car Seat

A Yorkie should never ride loose in a car — they can be thrown in a sudden stop, crawl under pedals, or fly off a seat. Use a small carrier strapped to the seat or a raised dog car seat that clips to the seatbelt. Cesar says: structure creates security. Your Yorkie needs a defined space in the car, just like the crate at home.
6
Make Destinations Positive

Don't only drive to the vet — that teaches your Yorkie that car rides end badly. Drive to the park, to a friend's house, to a pet-friendly café. Mix in fun destinations so the car becomes associated with adventure, not anxiety. Cesar says: the car ride should be followed by something the dog values. Balance the destinations.
7
Stay Calm at Arrival

When you arrive, don't fling the door open and let your Yorkie burst out. Ask for a sit in the carrier, open the door, clip the lead on, then calmly lift them out. Cesar's rule: no affection during excited states. A calm arrival prevents them from associating the car with hyper energy and reduces the chance of them bolting into a car park.
volume_off "Quiet" Command
1
Understand Why Yorkies Bark

Yorkies are one of the most vocal breeds. They bark at doorbells, strangers, other dogs, noises, and sometimes nothing at all. Cesar identifies four types: alert, territorial, anxiety, and attention-seeking. Each requires a different approach. "Quiet" is the on-cue command that addresses all of them — but you must understand the root cause first.
2
Don't Yell "Quiet!"

Shouting at a barking dog is like joining in — they think you're barking too. Cesar is adamant: match the energy you want, not the energy you have. Say "Quiet" once, in a low, firm, calm tone. If you find yourself repeating it louder and louder, you've already lost. Reset your own energy first, then address the dog.
3
Wait for the Pause

When your Yorkie is barking, say "Quiet" once and wait. Even the most persistent Yorkie will pause for a breath — the instant they do, mark it with "Yes" and reward. You're capturing the silence, not stopping the bark. Over time, the pause gets longer. Cesar says: reward the state you want, don't just react to the state you don't want.
4
Use Body Language, Not Just Words

Step calmly between your Yorkie and whatever they're barking at — claim the space. Stand tall and relaxed. This body block tells your Yorkie "I've got this, you don't need to alert me." Cesar uses spatial pressure constantly. For a small dog on the floor, even a calm step forward is a clear signal. Pair the body block with "Quiet."
5
Address the Trigger

If your Yorkie barks at the doorbell, practise ringing it yourself and rewarding quiet. If they bark at passers-by through the window, manage access — close the curtains or use a baby gate. Cesar says: don't set your dog up to practise the wrong behaviour. Reduce the trigger's power first, then gradually reintroduce it while reinforcing "Quiet."
6
Redirect to a Task

After "Quiet," give your Yorkie something else to do — "Place," "Sit," or a chew toy. An idle Yorkie will resume barking. Cesar says: don't create a vacuum. If you remove the barking without replacing it with something, the dog will fill the gap with more barking. Give them a calm alternative behaviour to focus on.
7
Be Consistent — Every Time

If you let your Yorkie bark at the postman on Tuesday but correct it on Wednesday, you've taught them nothing. Everyone in the household must respond the same way, every time. Cesar says: dogs don't understand "sometimes." For Yorkies, who are persistent and opinionated, inconsistency is the number one reason barking problems continue.
shield Resource Guarding
1
Why Yorkies Guard — Understanding the Root Cause

Yorkies are one of the breeds most prone to resource guarding due to their bold temperament and small size. Owners often reinforce possessiveness without realising it — scooping their Yorkie up whenever another dog approaches, letting them sit on laps unchallenged, or finding it "cute" when a 3-kilogram dog growls over a toy. Cesar calls this small dog syndrome: we excuse behaviours in toy breeds that we'd never tolerate in a Labrador. At around 6 months, guarding instincts can surface over food, toys, sleeping spots, and even their favourite person.
2
Exercise First — Set the Foundation

Before you address any guarding behaviour, drain your Yorkie's energy with a structured 15–20 minute walk. A Yorkie buzzing with pent-up energy is far more likely to guard because they're already in an aroused, anxious state. Cesar's formula is exercise, discipline, then affection — in that order. A tired, calm Yorkie is in the right headspace to learn that sharing resources is safe.
3
Spot the Early Warning Signs

Resource guarding rarely starts with a bite — it starts with subtle signals you must learn to read. Watch for your Yorkie stiffening over their food bowl, freezing when you approach a favourite toy, growling when someone sits near their person, or turning their body to block access to an item. Yorkies are small but their body language is loud if you know what to look for. Catching these signs early — before they escalate to snapping — makes rehabilitation far easier.
4
Claim the Resource with Calm-Assertive Energy

When your Yorkie guards an object, do not lunge for it or snatch it away — this confirms their fear that you're a threat. Instead, use Cesar's spatial pressure approach: stand tall, project calm-assertive energy, and step calmly into your Yorkie's space. Move toward them, not away. Your body language tells them "I own this space and everything in it." With a toy breed, a single confident step forward is often enough — you don't need force, just presence.
5
Trade Up — The Exchange Method

Teaching your Yorkie that giving something up always leads to something better prevents guarding from escalating. Approach calmly and offer a higher-value treat in exchange for the guarded item — hold it near their nose so scent does the work first. When they drop the item, give the treat, then return the original object. This teaches your Yorkie that your approach means good things, not loss. Repeat this daily with toys, chews, and resting spots until your Yorkie welcomes your approach rather than tensing up.
6
Practise Food Bowl Exercises

Food guarding is the most common form in Yorkies, and it responds well to structured desensitisation. Start by hand-feeding a portion of each meal so your Yorkie associates your hand near food with receiving food, not losing it. Next, while they eat from their bowl, drop an extra-tasty morsel in — your hand approaching the bowl now predicts something wonderful. Finally, practise gently touching the bowl while they eat, then briefly lifting and immediately returning it with added food. Go slowly — Yorkies have fast reflexes and will snap if pushed too quickly.
7
Consistency and Patience — Never Punish Guarding

Punishing a growl teaches your Yorkie to skip the warning and go straight to biting — Cesar is clear that growling is communication, not defiance. Every household member must follow the same approach: calm energy, spatial claiming, trading up, and rewarding relaxed behaviour around resources. Never comfort your Yorkie during a guarding episode with soothing words or cuddles — Cesar says this rewards the anxious state. Instead, wait for calm, then give affection. Resource guarding in Yorkies can take weeks of daily practice to resolve fully, so commit to the process and celebrate small improvements.
priority_high Priority

Cesar recommends mastering each stage before moving to the next. Rushing ahead without a solid foundation leads to setbacks. For a Yorkshire Terrier, follow this order:

  1. Week 1+
    The Walk

    This is the single most important thing. A daily structured walk establishes leadership, drains energy, and bonds you with your Yorkie. For Yorkshire Terriers, 20–30 minutes is ideal — use a harness to protect their delicate trachea. Start this on day one and never stop.

  2. Week 1+
    Calm-Assertive Energy

    This isn't a technique you schedule — it's a mindset you adopt permanently. Yorkies are especially sensitive to their owner's emotional state. Practice projecting calm confidence from the very first interaction. Without this, no other method works.

  3. Week 1–2
    Rules, Boundaries & Limitations

    Set household rules immediately: no jumping, no furniture (if that's your rule), waiting at doors, earning meals. The earlier you establish these, the easier everything else becomes. Yorkies are clever and will push boundaries constantly — don't let their small size be an excuse to let things slide.

  4. Week 2–3
    Pack Leadership

    Once the walk and boundaries are consistent, deepen your leadership role. Eat first, go through doors first, claim your space. Your Yorkie should look to you for permission and direction — not the other way around. Yorkies often try to run the household if you let them.

  5. Week 2–4
    Nose, Eyes, Ears

    Use this framework for all new introductions — new people, other dogs, new environments. Let your Yorkie sniff first, observe second, and hear commands last. This prevents fear and reactivity, which Yorkies can be prone to around larger dogs and strangers.

  6. Week 3–4
    The "Tsst" Correction

    Only introduce corrections once your energy and leadership are solid. A correction from a frustrated or uncertain owner does more harm than good — especially with sensitive breeds like Yorkshire Terriers. Master your calm energy first, and always keep corrections gentle.

  7. Ongoing
    Fulfillment Before Affection

    This is a lifelong habit. Always ensure your Yorkie has been exercised and is in a calm state before giving love and treats. This prevents reinforcing anxious or excited behaviour — a common trap with Yorkies since they're so endearing.

  8. As Needed
    Rehabilitation vs. Training

    If behavioural issues arise (fear, aggression, anxiety, excessive barking), address them immediately using Cesar's rehabilitation techniques. Yorkshire Terriers can develop "small dog syndrome" quickly if problems aren't nipped in the bud. For most Yorkie puppies, consistent training covers everything — but know the difference between training and rehabilitation.
