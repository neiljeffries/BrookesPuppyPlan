Cesar Millan's Training Methods

Proven techniques from the Dog Whisperer to raise a calm, balanced Yorkshire Terrier.

psychology Philosophy
psychology Core Philosophy Exercise, Discipline, Affection — in that order

Cesar's approach centres on understanding dogs as pack animals. Even a small Yorkshire Terrier needs a calm, assertive leader who provides structure and boundaries before affection. The formula is simple:

  1. Exercise — Drain your Yorkie's energy first through walks and play.
  2. Discipline — Set rules, boundaries, and limitations consistently — size doesn't excuse bad behaviour.
  3. Affection — Reward calm, submissive behaviour with love and treats.

library_books Methods
directions_walk The Walk

The structured walk is the foundation of Cesar's method. Walk your Yorkie beside or behind you — never in front. Even though they're small, letting them lead creates "small dog syndrome."

  • Use a lightweight harness to protect their delicate trachea
  • Keep a calm, forward-moving pace suited to their short stride
  • Correct pulling with a gentle sideways redirect
  • Walk for 20–30 minutes daily — Yorkies are energetic but tire faster than large breeds
 self_improvement Calm-Assertive Energy

Dogs mirror the energy of their owners. Yorkies are especially sensitive to your emotional state — Cesar teaches that projecting calm, assertive energy is the key to leadership. Anxious or excited energy creates an anxious or yappy Yorkie.

  • Stand tall with relaxed shoulders
  • Use steady, low-toned voice commands
  • Never correct your puppy when you're frustrated
  • Breathe and centre yourself before training sessions
 block Rules, Boundaries & Limitations

Dogs thrive with clear structure. Yorkies especially need consistent household rules from day one — their small size often leads owners to let bad behaviour slide, which creates bigger problems later.

  • Decide on no-go zones (furniture, certain rooms) — don't make exceptions because they're small
  • Make your Yorkie wait before meals and doorways
  • Don't allow jumping, nipping, or demand barking — common Yorkie habits to nip early
  • Every family member must enforce the same rules
 touch_app The "Tsst" Correction

Cesar's signature correction is a quick "tsst" sound paired with a calm finger touch to the neck or side. With a small breed like a Yorkie, be especially gentle — a light touch is all that's needed to interrupt their state.

  • Time the correction at the exact moment of the unwanted behaviour
  • Use minimal force — it's about interrupting the state, not punishment
  • Follow up by redirecting to a desired behaviour
  • Never use corrections when angry or emotional
 explore Nose, Eyes, Ears

Dogs experience the world in this order: nose first, then eyes, then ears. Cesar uses this to structure introductions and training.

  • Let your puppy sniff new things before expecting a reaction
  • Don't force eye contact — let it come naturally
  • Verbal commands are last, not first
  • Use scent-based enrichment to tire your puppy mentally
 group Pack Leadership

In Cesar's view, every household is a pack and your Yorkie needs to know their place in it. A Yorkie without a leader becomes yappy, possessive, or anxious — classic "small dog syndrome."

  • Eat before your dog and control food access
  • Enter and exit spaces first
  • Don't comfort fearful behaviour — redirect it
  • Claim your space by standing tall and stepping forward
 spa Rehabilitation vs. Training

Cesar distinguishes between training (teaching commands) and rehabilitation (fixing behavioural issues). Yorkie puppies mostly need training, but bad habits like excessive barking and resource guarding can start early.

  • Address fear, aggression, or anxiety immediately
  • Don't reinforce unwanted states with soothing words
  • Flooding (controlled exposure) can help with fearfulness
  • Patience and consistency are more important than any technique
 volunteer_activism Fulfillment Before Affection

Cesar stresses that giving affection at the wrong time can reinforce bad behaviour. Your Yorkie needs to feel fulfilled through exercise and mental stimulation before receiving love — no matter how adorable they look.

  • No affection during excited, anxious, or aggressive states
  • Reward calm, relaxed body language with gentle praise
  • Use treats sparingly — connection matters more than food
  • Quality time (calm sitting together) is the best reward
format_list_numbered Steps
1
Know Your Dog's Energy Level

Before any training begins, observe your Yorkie's energy level. Yorkshire Terriers are typically medium-to-high energy despite their small size. They're feisty, alert, and easily excited. This means they often need more mental stimulation than physical exhaustion before they can focus.
2
Exercise First — Always

Take your Yorkie on a structured walk before every training session. A tired Yorkie is a focused Yorkie. This drains excess energy and puts them in a calm, receptive state. For a Yorkshire Terrier, 15–20 minutes of brisk walking is usually enough — don't overdo it with their small legs.
3
Project Calm-Assertive Energy

Centre yourself before engaging. Stand tall, breathe slowly, and keep your body relaxed but confident. Your Yorkie reads your body language and emotional state before anything else. Yorkies are exceptionally attuned to their owner's mood — if you're tense or frustrated, they'll become anxious or defiant.
4
Set Clear Rules & Boundaries

Decide on the specific behaviour you want to address. Communicate the boundary through body language and spatial pressure — step forward, claim your space, and use a calm "tsst" to interrupt unwanted behaviour. Be consistent: the rule is always the rule, not just sometimes.
5
Correct at the Right Moment

Timing is everything. Correct your Yorkie at the exact instant the unwanted behaviour starts — not after. A late correction confuses any dog. With Yorkies, use the lightest correction possible: a soft sound or gentle spatial pressure. Their small size means a little goes a long way.
6
Redirect to the Desired Behaviour

After correcting, immediately guide your puppy toward what you do want. If they were jumping, ask for a sit. If they were pulling, redirect their focus forward. Nature abhors a vacuum — don't just stop a behaviour, replace it with the right one.
7
Wait for the Calm State

Do not reward until your Yorkie is in a calm, submissive state. Watch for relaxed ears, a low or neutral tail, soft eyes, and a loose body. This is the moment your dog is truly learning. Yorkies are expert manipulators with their cute faces — don't let puppy eyes fool you into rewarding an excited or demanding state.
8
Reward with Affection

Once your Yorkie is calm and balanced, reward them with gentle affection — a soft touch, calm praise, or a tiny treat. This is Cesar's third pillar. Affection given at the right time seals the lesson and strengthens the bond between you and your Yorkie.
9
Repeat with Patience & Consistency

Dogs learn through repetition, not explanation. Practice the same routine daily. Every family member must follow the same rules — Yorkies are smart enough to test each person for weak spots. Cesar says it takes a dog as little as 10 minutes to learn a new behaviour — but it takes humans much longer to be consistent enough to make it stick.
menu_book Guides
front_hand Sit
1
Exercise First

Take your Yorkie on a brisk 15–20 minute walk before the session. A dog full of pent-up energy cannot focus. You need your Yorkie in a calm, receptive state before asking for any command. Even though they're small, Yorkshire Terriers have plenty of energy to burn.
2
Claim Your Space

Stand tall and calm. Project calm-assertive energy. Your Yorkie should already be looking to you for direction. If they're distracted or hyper, they're not ready — walk more or wait. Yorkies can be stubborn, so patience here is key.
3
Use Body Language First

Hold a tiny treat above and slightly behind your Yorkie's head. Their natural response is to look up and sit down. Do not say "sit" yet — let the body movement happen first. Use pea-sized treats — Yorkies are tiny and can gain weight quickly. Cesar teaches nose, eyes, ears: the dog experiences the scent and visual cue before the verbal one.
4
Mark the Moment

The instant their bottom touches the ground, give calm praise — a gentle "good" — and offer the treat. Timing is critical. Rewarding even one second late teaches the wrong thing.
5
Add the Word

Once your Yorkie reliably sits from the hand gesture alone (after several sessions), add the word "sit" just before the hand motion. The word becomes associated with the action they already know. Never repeat the command — say it once, wait, and guide if needed. Yorkies are clever and will learn to ignore repeated commands quickly.
6
Phase Out Treats

Gradually reduce treats and replace with calm affection. Cesar emphasises that the connection with the owner should be the ultimate reward, not food. Yorkies can become treat-obsessed, so transition to praise and gentle touch as soon as the behaviour is consistent.
pan_tool Stay
1
Master "Sit" First

"Stay" builds on "sit." Your Yorkie must be able to sit reliably on command before attempting this. If the foundation isn't solid, go back to the sit training. Yorkies are naturally restless, so "stay" requires extra patience.
2
Ask for Sit, Then Hold

Ask your Yorkie to sit. Once they're sitting calmly, hold your open palm facing them (a "stop" gesture) and take one small step back. Wait just 2–3 seconds at first. If they stay, return and reward with calm affection.
3
Correct Breaks Immediately

If your Yorkie breaks the stay, use a calm "tsst" and guide them back to the exact spot. Do not show frustration. Reset and try again with a shorter duration. The correction must come at the instant they start to move, not after they've already gotten up. Be gentle — Yorkies are sensitive to harsh corrections.
4
Increase Duration Gradually

Add a few seconds at a time. Go from 3 seconds to 5, then 10, then 20. Over days, build up to minutes. Never rush — if your Yorkie fails repeatedly, you've advanced too quickly. Drop back to where they were succeeding.
5
Add Distance

Once your Yorkie holds a stay for 30+ seconds with you one step away, gradually increase your distance. Take two steps, then three, then walk across the room. Always return to your Yorkie to reward — never call them to you, as that teaches them to break the stay.
6
Add Distractions

The final challenge is proofing against distractions. Practice in different rooms, then outside. Have someone walk by. Drop a toy nearby. Each distraction tests your Yorkie's self-control. Correct breaks calmly and reset. A Yorkie that can stay amidst distractions is a balanced dog.
pets Come
1
Start Indoors with No Distractions

Begin in a quiet room with no competing stimuli. Your Yorkie should already be in a calm state — if they're wound up, take them on a walk first. Cesar says recall training fails outdoors when it hasn't been mastered indoors first. Yorkies have an independent streak, so start in the most controlled environment possible.
2
Use Body Language Before Words

Crouch down to your Yorkie's level and open your body posture — this is an invitation dogs understand instinctively. Tap your knees or clap gently. When your Yorkie moves toward you, then introduce the word "come." Nose, eyes, ears — the physical cue comes first, the verbal cue last.
3
Reward the Arrival — Every Time

The instant your Yorkie reaches you, give calm praise and a tiny treat. Coming to you must always be the best thing that happens to them. Cesar warns: never call your dog to you and then do something they dislike (bath, crate, leaving the park). That teaches them "come" means something bad happens.
4
Add Distance Gradually

Start just a few feet away. Once your Yorkie comes reliably at close range, move to the other side of the room, then another room, then a hallway. Each increase in distance is a new challenge — don't skip steps. If they start ignoring you, you've gone too far too fast. Yorkies can be wilful, so keep sessions short (5–10 minutes) to maintain engagement.
5
Use a Long Line Outdoors

When you move outside, use a 15–20 foot training lead (lighter weight for a small breed). This gives your Yorkie the feeling of freedom while you maintain control. Let them explore, then call "come" once — calmly and clearly. If they don't respond, gently guide them in with the line. Never chase your Yorkie — that turns it into a game they'll always win.
6
Never Repeat the Command

Cesar is adamant: say "come" once. If you say it five times, you're teaching your dog that the first four don't count. Say it once, wait, and if they don't respond, use the long line to guide them. One command, one response — that's the standard.
7
Proof Against Distractions

The ultimate test is calling your Yorkie away from something exciting — another dog, food on the ground, a squirrel. Build up to this slowly. Practice near mild distractions first, then gradually increase the difficulty. A reliable recall in any situation could save your Yorkie's life — especially important for a small breed that's vulnerable to larger animals and traffic.
volume_off Stop Barking
1
Identify the Cause

Cesar says barking is a symptom, not the problem. Yorkies are one of the most vocal breeds — they bark from boredom, anxiety, territorial instinct, excitement, or simply because they can. Each root cause needs a different energy from you. An under-exercised Yorkie barks because they have no outlet — the fix is the walk, not correcting the bark.
2
Exercise to Drain Energy

Most nuisance barking stems from excess energy. A well-exercised Yorkie is a quieter Yorkie. Before addressing the barking itself, make sure your Yorkie is getting sufficient daily exercise — 15–25 minutes of walking plus indoor play. A tired Yorkie has far less to bark about.
3
Stay Calm — Never Yell

Yelling at a barking Yorkie is the worst thing you can do. Your Yorkie interprets your shouting as you joining in — and Yorkies love a good bark-along. Project calm-assertive energy. Your silence and composed body language will do more than any loud command.
4
Claim the Trigger

If your Yorkie barks at the door or window, step between them and the trigger. Claim that space with your body. Move toward your Yorkie, not away. Use a calm "tsst" and spatial pressure to push them back from the trigger. You're telling them: "I've got this, you don't need to alert." Yorkies often appoint themselves as guard dogs — let them know that's your job.
5
Wait for Silence, Then Reward

Do not give any attention — positive or negative — while your Yorkie is barking. Wait for even a brief moment of silence. The instant they stop, reward with calm praise. You're teaching them that silence earns attention, barking earns nothing. With Yorkies, this takes real commitment — they can bark for a long time.
6
Be Consistent Every Time

Every family member must respond the same way. If one person yells and another ignores it, your Yorkie gets mixed signals. Consistency is the key to breaking the cycle. Cesar says most barking problems are resolved within days when the entire household commits to calm, consistent correction — but with Yorkies, expect it to take a bit longer. They're tenacious.
do_not_step Stop Jumping
1
Understand Why They Jump

Yorkies jump to seek attention and to greet at face level — and because they're so small, people often pick them up or laugh, which rewards the behaviour. When you respond with excitement, pushing, or even telling them "no" in a high-pitched voice, you're reinforcing the jumping. Cesar teaches that jumping is almost always the owner's fault — we've trained them that jumping works.
2
No Touch, No Talk, No Eye Contact

When your Yorkie jumps on you, give them absolutely nothing. Turn your body sideways, fold your arms, look away, and say nothing. This is Cesar's "no touch, no talk, no eye contact" rule. You become boring. The jumping loses its payoff. This is especially hard with Yorkies because they're adorable — but resist.
3
Claim Your Space

If turning away isn't enough, step calmly into your Yorkie's space. Move forward, not backward. Use your body to push them back without touching them. A calm "tsst" at the moment they begin to leap interrupts the behaviour before it completes. Be gentle with your movement — they're small and can be knocked over easily.
4
Wait for Four on the Floor

Only greet your Yorkie when all four paws are on the ground and they are in a calm state. This may take patience — they may jump several times before giving up. The moment they stand or sit calmly, get low to their level, and give gentle, calm affection. You're teaching them: "calm gets love, jumping gets nothing."
5
Practice with Guests

The hardest test is when visitors arrive. Ask guests to follow the same rules: ignore the jumping, turn away, no excited greetings. Put your Yorkie on a leash during arrivals if needed. Make them sit before the guest engages. Cesar says the first 5 minutes of a guest's arrival determine the dog's behaviour for the entire visit. Warn guests not to "baby" your Yorkie — this undoes all your work.
6
Exercise Before Social Situations

If you know guests are coming, take your Yorkie on a walk beforehand. A well-exercised Yorkie has far less impulse to jump. Combine this with the "no touch, no talk, no eye contact" rule, and jumping disappears within weeks.
grass Potty Training
1
Establish a Routine

Cesar emphasises that dogs are creatures of routine. Take your Yorkie outside at the same times every day: first thing in the morning, after meals, after naps, after play, and last thing at night. Yorkies have tiny bladders, so take them out more frequently than you would a larger breed — every 1–2 hours for young pups.
2
Pick a Designated Spot

Always take your Yorkie to the same area outside. The scent from previous visits tells them "this is the bathroom." Walk them on a leash to the spot — don't let them wander and play first. This is a business trip, not a field trip. In bad weather, Yorkies are more likely to refuse to go outside, so be firm and consistent.
3
Reward Immediately

The instant your Yorkie finishes going in the right spot, give calm praise and a tiny treat. Not 10 seconds later — immediately. They need to connect the act of going in that spot with the reward. Cesar says timing is everything in dog psychology.
4
Supervise Constantly Indoors

When inside, keep your Yorkie in the same room as you. Watch for signs they need to go: sniffing the floor, circling, squatting. The moment you see these signs, calmly pick them up and carry them outside to the designated spot. Yorkies are small enough to scoop up quickly — use this to your advantage. Prevention is easier than correction.
5
Never Punish Accidents

Cesar is clear: never rub a dog's nose in it or yell after an accident. If you didn't catch them in the act, the moment has passed — punishment after the fact creates fear and confusion, not learning. Clean it up with an enzymatic cleaner (critical for Yorkies — if they can still smell it, they'll go there again) and recommit to supervision.
6
Use Crate Training as a Tool

Dogs instinctively avoid soiling their sleeping area. A properly sized crate for your Yorkie (big enough to stand and turn, not bigger) helps develop bladder control. Take them straight outside when you open the crate. Cesar views the crate as a den — a safe, natural space — never as punishment. For Yorkies, ensure the crate isn't too large or they may designate one corner as a toilet.
7
Use a Training Mat as a Backup

A puppy training mat (or pee pad) can be a valuable indoor safety net — especially for a tiny breed like a Yorkie. Place the mat in one consistent spot away from their sleeping and eating areas. Treat it the same way you treat the outdoor spot: lead your Yorkie to the mat on a schedule, wait calmly, and reward the instant they use it.

  • Use a mat with an absorbent core and leak-proof backing to protect your floors
  • Keep the mat in the same location — moving it confuses your puppy
  • Transition gradually from mat to outdoor-only by moving the mat closer to the door over time, then removing it once outdoor habits are solid
  • A mat is especially useful overnight, during bad weather, or when you can't get outside quickly enough for a tiny Yorkie bladder
  • Never use the mat as a permanent replacement for outdoor training — the goal is always to get your Yorkie going outside reliably
8
Be Patient — It Takes Weeks

Yorkshire Terriers are notoriously one of the hardest breeds to house-train. Most Yorkies take 4–6 months or even longer to be fully reliable. Cesar reminds owners that setbacks are normal and not a sign of failure. Stay calm, stay consistent, and trust the process. Small bladders and stubborn temperaments mean this takes longer than with larger breeds — but it absolutely can be done.
directions_walk Heel Walk
1
Practice Leash Skills Indoors First

Before you ever step outside, clip the leash on inside the house. Walk your Yorkie through hallways and around rooms. This low-distraction environment is where they learn to follow your lead without competing smells, sounds, or other dogs. Cesar says if your dog can't walk beside you in the living room, they're not ready for the street.
2
Master the Door Ritual

The walk starts before you open the front door. Put the leash on calmly — if your Yorkie goes wild with excitement, stand still and wait. Do not open the door until they are sitting or standing calmly beside you. Then you walk through first, and invite them to follow. Cesar says whoever goes through the door first leads the walk — it must always be you.
3
Claim the Hallway & Indoor Spaces

Walk through every doorway and narrow space in your home first. Change direction without warning. Stop suddenly and wait for your Yorkie to stop and look at you. These indoor exercises build the habit of paying attention to you and establish that you control the movement. Once your Yorkie follows you calmly through the house without pulling, lunging, or forging ahead, they're ready for the real walk.
4
Understand the Pack Walk

In Cesar's world, the walk is the single most important activity. A Yorkie that walks beside or behind you is acknowledging you as the pack leader. A Yorkie that pulls ahead believes they are leading — and Yorkies definitely think they're bigger than they are. This isn't about obedience — it's about the relationship dynamic.
5
Use the Right Equipment

For a Yorkshire Terrier, use a well-fitted harness rather than a collar — Yorkies are prone to tracheal collapse, and any pressure on the neck can be dangerous. Keep the leash short for control, but avoid tension. A lightweight leash works best for their small frame. Never use a retractable leash — these teach pulling.
6
Set the Pace — You Lead

You decide the speed, direction, and when to stop. Walk with purpose and confidence. Don't let your Yorkie dictate the route by sniffing every bush. Sniff breaks are earned at your discretion. Keep in mind your Yorkie's small stride — slow your pace slightly so they can walk comfortably beside you without having to trot.
7
Correct Pulling Instantly

The moment the leash goes tight, give a gentle sideways redirect (not a tug — Yorkies are delicate) and a calm "tsst." Then immediately resume walking. Don't stop and wait — keep moving. The correction should snap your Yorkie out of the pulling state, not yank them back. Be mindful of their small size — corrections should be light.
8
Change Directions Frequently

Keep your Yorkie guessing by changing direction often — especially at first. Turn left, turn right, do a U-turn. This forces your Yorkie to pay attention to you rather than the environment. When they start watching you for cues, you've gained their focus.
9
Reward the Heel Position

When your Yorkie walks calmly beside you with a loose leash, give quiet praise or an occasional tiny treat at your side. Don't make a big fuss — calm energy reinforces calm behaviour. They should learn that being next to you is the most comfortable, rewarding place to be.
10
Practice Daily — No Shortcuts

Cesar says the walk must happen every single day, rain or shine. It's not optional exercise — it's the core of your leadership. For a Yorkshire Terrier, aim for 20–30 minutes of structured walking per day. Their small legs cover less ground, but the mental benefits of a daily pack walk are the same as for any breed.
priority_high Priority

Cesar recommends mastering each stage before moving to the next. Rushing ahead without a solid foundation leads to setbacks. For a Yorkshire Terrier, follow this order:

  1. Week 1+
    The Walk

    This is the single most important thing. A daily structured walk establishes leadership, drains energy, and bonds you with your Yorkie. For Yorkshire Terriers, 20–30 minutes is ideal — use a harness to protect their delicate trachea. Start this on day one and never stop.

  2. Week 1+
    Calm-Assertive Energy

    This isn't a technique you schedule — it's a mindset you adopt permanently. Yorkies are especially sensitive to their owner's emotional state. Practice projecting calm confidence from the very first interaction. Without this, no other method works.

  3. Week 1–2
    Rules, Boundaries & Limitations

    Set household rules immediately: no jumping, no furniture (if that's your rule), waiting at doors, earning meals. The earlier you establish these, the easier everything else becomes. Yorkies are clever and will push boundaries constantly — don't let their small size be an excuse to let things slide.

  4. Week 2–3
    Pack Leadership

    Once the walk and boundaries are consistent, deepen your leadership role. Eat first, go through doors first, claim your space. Your Yorkie should look to you for permission and direction — not the other way around. Yorkies often try to run the household if you let them.

  5. Week 2–4
    Nose, Eyes, Ears

    Use this framework for all new introductions — new people, other dogs, new environments. Let your Yorkie sniff first, observe second, and hear commands last. This prevents fear and reactivity, which Yorkies can be prone to around larger dogs and strangers.

  6. Week 3–4
    The "Tsst" Correction

    Only introduce corrections once your energy and leadership are solid. A correction from a frustrated or uncertain owner does more harm than good — especially with sensitive breeds like Yorkshire Terriers. Master your calm energy first, and always keep corrections gentle.

  7. Ongoing
    Fulfillment Before Affection

    This is a lifelong habit. Always ensure your Yorkie has been exercised and is in a calm state before giving love and treats. This prevents reinforcing anxious or excited behaviour — a common trap with Yorkies since they're so endearing.

  8. As Needed
    Rehabilitation vs. Training

    If behavioural issues arise (fear, aggression, anxiety, excessive barking), address them immediately using Cesar's rehabilitation techniques. Yorkshire Terriers can develop "small dog syndrome" quickly if problems aren't nipped in the bud. For most Yorkie puppies, consistent training covers everything — but know the difference between training and rehabilitation.
